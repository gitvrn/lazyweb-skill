Lifting annual-plan share on a hard paywall — without tanking trial & subscribe rate

A growth-PM research brief for a top-tier (Duolingo-class) language-learning app. Goal: shift the mix toward the annual plan on a hard paywall, while the overall trial-start / subscribe rate holds as a guardrail. Evidence is drawn from Lazyweb's lazyweb_ab_test_research experiment set (PRO) and paired with real mobile paywall screenshots from the Lazyweb library.

What the evidence says (TL;DR)

Across 15 high-confidence paywall experiments, the apps that pushed users toward the annual plan rarely did it by removing the monthly option. They did it by changing the frame: pre-selecting annual, anchoring it against a visible monthly (or lifetime) price, and converting the free trial from a separate decision into a risk-reversal lever attached to the annual plan. That last move is exactly what protects your guardrail — the trial keeps the overall start rate up while annual becomes the default destination.

The lever that moves mix

Default selection + savings framing (BEST VALUE, "Save 60%", per-month math). Steers attention to annual value before the user even taps.

The lever that protects the guardrail

Free trial as a toggle on the annual plan, not the headline choice. Keeps trial-start rate high while annual is pre-selected.

The risk to watch

Higher-ARPU anchors (lifetime) can boost annual feel — but watch downstream refunds/cancellation, not just the tap.

Evidence honesty: Lazyweb's _experiments is a screenshot-diff evidence set — it captures what shipped between two captures and a reasoned hypothesis, not a statistically measured lift readout. There is no quantified win/loss or significance in this data. Treat every "learning" below as a directional hypothesis with a confidence label, and rank your own tests by your own readouts.

Drops  — the closest analog Education · Language Learning high design bar confidence: high
What changed
The plan selector changed from three comparable Yearly, Lifetime, and Monthly cards to two purchase choices that put a $199.99 one-time purchase above a discounted yearly trial, with Yearly selected and a prominent Start Free Trial CTA.
Hypothesis (learning)
Leading with a lifetime anchor while selecting a discounted yearly trial can make the annual trial feel cheaper and easier to start — likely improving trial starts without removing the higher-ARPU option.
Why it matters for your goal
This is the cleanest "annual-share ↑ with the guardrail intact" pattern in the set: annual is the default, the trial protects start-rate, and the lifetime sits above annual purely as an anchor.

Target metric, guardrail metric & the supporting set

Per the brief — annual-plan mix is the win condition; trial/subscribe rate must not regress. The experiment records themselves flagged "increase annual plan share" as the target and downstream retention / refund-and-support signal as the guardrail, which aligns with the brief and is worth instrumenting from day one.

Target Annual-plan share of new subscriptions Annual purchases ÷ all new paid conversions (incl. trial-starts that convert). The single number this work is trying to move.
Guardrail Overall trial-start / subscribe rate (paywall → any paid intent) Hard stop. If a variant lifts annual share but drops total conversions, mix is "won" on a smaller base — net-negative. Ship only if this is flat or up.

Recommended test sequence

Ordered by expected mix impact ÷ guardrail risk. Run sequentially (or in a shared holdout) so learnings compound; do not bundle Steps 1–3 into one variant or you won't know which lever moved the number. Each step names the experiment/reference it leans on.

Pre-select annual + add a strong monthly anchor & per-month math

Keep all current plans, but make annual the default selection, show the monthly price as the explicit anchor, and surface the annual "per-month" equivalent with a savings badge. Lowest-risk, highest-evidence move — it changes framing, not the offer.
Leans on: Acrobat (annual selected + stronger monthly anchor) · Instacart / Quizlet (per-month math, "best value"). Guardrail risk: low.

Convert the free trial into a toggle on the annual plan (not a separate choice)

Make the trial a risk-reversal lever attached to annual with a visible reminder→bill-date timeline, instead of "trial vs paid" being the headline decision. This is the move that protects the guardrail: trial-start stays easy while annual is the destination.
Leans on: FaceApp (trial toggle on annual) · Quizlet / Epic (trial-works timeline + reminder). Guardrail risk: low–medium (watch trial-start rate directly).

Reframe the headline to percentage savings + "BEST VALUE" badge on annual

Move from price-led to savings-led ("Save 60%", "2 months free") with a BEST VALUE badge on annual and light social proof. Shifts perceived value toward the longer plan.
Leans on: Snapchat+ ("Save 60%" + BEST VALUE + tiers + social proof) · Night Sky (% off annual). Guardrail risk: medium — savings framing can occasionally suppress monthly-only buyers; watch total conversion.

Add a higher-ARPU anchor above annual (lifetime) — only if Steps 1–3 hold the guardrail

Introduce a lifetime (or top-tier) anchor priced above annual so the annual trial "feels cheaper and easier to start." Powerful for mix, but the experiment data warns it can trade short-term mix for downstream refunds.
Leans on: Drops (lifetime anchor + selected discounted yearly — the closest in-category analog) · Habits (Plus/lifetime/bundle tiering). Guardrail risk: medium–high — instrument refunds & early cancels, not just the tap.

Tighten value clarity on the annual offer (benefit rows / branded header / trimmed bullets)

A polish pass on the same annual offer: a branded header, concrete benefit rows, and a shorter, more legible bullet list. Low-risk reinforcement that helps the trial CTA convert once mix is shifted.
Leans on: MyFitnessPal (branded header + benefit rows) · Claude (trimmed bullets, same annual price). Guardrail risk: low.
Anti-pattern to test against, not toward: Facetune / onX Hunt / Hallow shipped lower-duration / cheaper-monthly entry plans to reduce commitment anxiety and capture price-sensitive users. That lifts start rate but works against annual share. If your guardrail is wobbling, these are the levers you'd pull back, not add — e.g. a $0.99 monthly (Hallow) is exactly the kind of cheap fallback that can quietly cannibalize annual mix.

Evidence strength & gaps

What's strong

  • Volume & consistency. 15 high-confidence experiments, and the annual-default / monthly-anchor / trial-as-toggle pattern recurs across unrelated categories — that convergence is the real signal.
  • An in-category, high-design-bar analog. Drops is language-learning and flagged high-design-bar, doing almost exactly the move your goal implies.
  • Mechanics, not just looks. Each record names the structural change (selection default, anchor, trial placement), so the hypotheses are transferable rather than cosmetic.
  • Matched visual references. Every recommended lever has a real mobile screenshot showing it in production (Drops, Speak, Quizlet, Epic, Instacart, Snapchat-style savings).

What's weak / missing

  • No measured lift. _experiments is screenshot-diff evidence — "what shipped" + a reasoned hypothesis, not a win/loss readout with significance. Confidence labels describe the detection, not an outcome.
  • No mix numbers. Nothing here quantifies how much annual share moved, or whether the guardrail held, in any of these apps. You must measure that yourself.
  • Few exact-category matches. Most transferable mechanics come from Photo/Health/Productivity/News apps; only Drops (and adjacent education apps) are truly in-category.
  • Platform field is sparse. Several records have platform: null; treat all as mobile-plausible given the corpus, but it's inferred.
  • Tier mechanic unmodeled. Duolingo-class apps often sell tiers (Super/Max) on top of duration. The evidence is about duration selection; your annual/monthly test must sit inside a tier, which these examples don't fully address.
  • Empty narrow query. A tightly-filtered first query (company="Duolingo-class…", category=Education) returned 0 rows; the useful set came from a broad query — a reminder that this corpus rewards loose retrieval.
Where not to overgeneralize. (1) These are hypotheses — pick your sequence by your own readouts, not by the count of apps that did a thing. (2) A move that raised revenue/subscriber elsewhere (Zeus price hike, LockWidget) is not the same as raising annual share, and can hurt your conversion guardrail. (3) The lifetime-anchor and savings-headline levers are the highest-mix-impact and highest-risk — they're sequenced last for a reason; do not ship them before the safe framing moves prove the guardrail holds. (4) Cheaper/shorter-duration entry plans (Facetune, Hallow $0.99 monthly) optimize a different objective (start rate / price-sensitivity) and will pull against annual mix.
