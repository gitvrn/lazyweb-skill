1 Target Paywall — Read & Diagnosis

No live capture was provided, so this is read from the brief and grounded against the closest real screens in the Lazyweb corpus — the Tinder Gold / Platinum upgrade family, which is the same archetype (full-screen, benefits list + plan cards, monthly default, see-who-likes-you trigger). Diagnosis below is what to instrument; treat it as the prior the hypotheses attack.

Layout
Full-screen interstitial · benefits/feature block over multi-tier plan cards · feature comparison grid
Components
Hero + value prop, feature list (see who likes you, boosts, unlimited likes, super likes, rewinds, passport), 2–3 tier cards, comparison grid, single primary CTA, restore + legal
Offer
Multi-tier (e.g. Gold / Platinum) · monthly default selected · per-period pricing · no/short trial
Strategic moves present
Locked-feature framing, tier framing, benefits enumeration
User state
Warm feature wall — user tapped a locked action (e.g. "see who liked you") and hit the wall with intent
Conversion goal
Primary: paid conversion. Secondary: tier mix shift up (Gold→Platinum) & annual-plan share

Current friction (hypothesized — to instrument)

  • Monthly default anchors low ARPU. A monthly-selected default with no visible annual savings leaves cheaper-per-month annual revenue on the table.
  • Flat benefits list dilutes the trigger feature. When "see who likes you" sits in a bullet list with 7 other perks, the one feature the user came for loses salience.
  • Tiers compete without a clear "lose this" frame. Side-by-side Gold/Platinum cards without a comparison grid that shows what the cheaper tier *forfeits* under-sell the upgrade.
  • Cold price, no risk reversal. Charging immediately (no trial / no intro) at a warm-but-unproven moment raises commitment anxiety vs. trial-led competitors.
  • The warm moment is wasted. Generic "Upgrade to Gold" copy ignores the live signal — the actual blurred faces / like count the user just tried to open.
Closest real-world analogue in the corpus. Tinder's own upgrade family appears repeatedly in Lazyweb (a Lazyweb cluster of 12+ near-identical Tinder paywall captures). It pairs a "see who likes you" feature-wall (blurred Likes inbox) with a benefits-list upgrade screen and a Gold plan selector — exactly the structure in this brief. We use it as both the reference and the realism check below.

3 Optimization Hypotheses

Four falsifiable hypotheses, each attacking a different named friction with a different mechanism. Form: Making [change] should [outcome] because [mechanism]. Evidence is labelled EXP (directional A/B) or REF (visual/convention). No measured lift is claimed — the experiment set is screenshot-diff, not validated magnitude.

Hypothesis 1 · Trigger-feature hero

Make the wall about the faces, not the feature list

Making the paywall lead with the user's own blurred Likes grid + live like-count as the hero (instead of opening on a flat 8-item benefits list) should lift paid conversion from the see-who-likes-you entry point because the screen reflects the exact thing the user just reached for, converting live intent instead of re-pitching the whole product.

Mechanism & friction attacked

Attacks "trigger feature diluted by the benefits list" and "wasted warm moment." Curiosity/loss-aversion is highest at the wall; surfacing the personalized blurred grid keeps the salient reward in view rather than abstracting it into a perk among many.

Expected metric

↑ paid conversion rate from the Likes/feature-wall entry; ↑ CTA tap-through on that surface

Implementation

  • Top 55–60% of screen = blurred Likes grid + "{N} people like you" counter (pulled live)
  • Single dominant benefit headline: "See who likes you"; demote other perks to a collapsed "+ everything in Gold" row
  • Keep plan cards below the fold; one primary CTA

Supporting evidence

  • REF Tinder feature wall (sc_id 8802) — the blurred-grid + count + "See who likes you" CTA is already a proven shipped pattern in-category.
  • EXP Reddit (sc_id 22940) & MyRadar (9201) — productized/concrete value beats broad VIP language; here the "product" is the user's own likes.

Risk

If the user has few/zero likes, a sparse grid reduces perceived value. Mitigate: gate the personalized hero on a like-count threshold; fall back to the benefits-led layout below it. Also a known existing Tinder convention — falsifiable test is the degree of dominance, not the mere presence of the grid.

Hypothesis 2 · Comparison grid with loss framing

Replace the flat benefits list with a "what you lose at the lower tier" grid

Making the feature block a three-column comparison grid (Free / Gold / Platinum) that explicitly shows the rows the cheaper tier leaves unchecked should shift tier mix upward (Gold→Platinum) and lift overall paid conversion because users evaluating side-by-side feel the loss of un-owned features more sharply than they value a flat bullet list.

Mechanism & friction attacked

Attacks "tiers compete without a 'lose this' frame." Loss aversion + the contrast effect: an explicit grid of red-X vs green-check rows makes the gap between tiers legible and reframes the upgrade as avoiding a loss, not paying for an extra.

Expected metric

↑ Platinum (top-tier) share of paid; ↑ paid conversion; watch ↓ in checkout abandonment from comparison clarity

Implementation

  • Rows = the high-intent features (see who likes you, weekly super likes, monthly boost, priority likes, passport)
  • Columns Free/Gold/Platinum with check / dash / X cells; pre-select the tier carrying the trigger feature
  • "Best value" or "Most popular" ribbon on the target tier

Supporting evidence

  • REF Weather Channel (13404) & AccuWeather (90544) — free-vs-premium / Premium-vs-Premium+ comparison grids in shipped paywalls.
  • REF Perplexity (65969) Free/Pro/Max + Washington Post (9184) two-tier checklists — the exact 3-column / tier-checklist structure.
  • EXP JustFit (8060) — adding plan comparison reduced subscribe-now friction.

Risk

A dense grid can overwhelm on a small screen and push the CTA below the fold, hurting conversion even while it helps tier mix. Mitigate: cap at ~5 rows, make the grid horizontally compact, keep CTA sticky. Test conversion and mix together so a mix win doesn't mask a conversion loss.

Hypothesis 3 · Annual anchoring & default

Anchor on annual: show per-week math + savings badge, keep monthly visible but un-defaulted

Making the plan cards default to annual with a "$X.XX/week, billed yearly" breakdown and a struck-through monthly-equivalent + "Save 50%" badge (rather than defaulting to monthly with no annual savings shown) should lift annual-plan share and blended ARPU because a high anchor and explicit per-week framing make the annual commitment feel small and the monthly option look expensive by comparison.

Mechanism & friction attacked

Attacks "monthly default anchors low ARPU." Price anchoring + unit-price reframing: the annual price stated as a tiny weekly number, anchored against a visibly higher monthly, makes annual the obvious value choice.

Expected metric

↑ annual-plan share of new paid; ↑ blended ARPU / LTV; neutral-to-up overall conversion

Implementation

  • Pre-select annual; render "$X/wk" large with "billed $YY/yr" small beneath
  • "Save 50% vs monthly" badge; show monthly's higher per-week number for contrast
  • Optional: nudge the monthly price up slightly to widen the anchor (per sc_id 23364)

Supporting evidence

  • EXP Arta (sc_id 23364) — holding annual steady while raising the weekly price made annual look better & lifted ARPU. Direct anchoring evidence.
  • REF Audible (74793) "best value" annual, Babbel (2852) save-badges + struck prices, Reader (90579) / Washington Post (9184) yearly savings badges.

Risk

Defaulting to a higher total price can depress first-purchase conversion among price-sensitive users — an annual-share win that costs total subscribers. Mitigate: monitor total paid conversion as a guardrail and consider an intro-priced first annual term. Dating churn is high, so optimize for first-term annual revenue, not headline LTV.

Hypothesis 4 · Risk reversal at the warm moment

Add a short, clearly-framed trial / intro term with the condition next to the CTA

Making the offer a 3-day trial (or low intro price) with the terms stated inline directly under the CTA ("Free for 3 days, then $YY/yr — cancel anytime") should lift paid starts (trial→paid) at the warm wall because risk reversal lowers commitment anxiety for a user with high intent but no proof the product will pay off.

Mechanism & friction attacked

Attacks "cold price / no risk reversal." Trial framing converts intent into a commitment with perceived zero downside; placing the condition next to the CTA removes the "what am I actually agreeing to?" hesitation.

Expected metric

↑ subscription starts at the wall; track trial→paid conversion as the true north so trials don't just inflate starts

Implementation

  • Short trial (3 days) or intro price on the entry tier; full price clearly shown
  • One line under the CTA: "Free for 3 days, then $YY/yr · cancel anytime"
  • Keep one primary CTA; avoid a separate "skip trial" path competing for the tap

Supporting evidence

  • EXP Quizlet (14752) & Arta (1410) — putting the trial condition next to the CTA reduced hesitation about the commitment.
  • EXP VPN (23350) & Mimo (8901) — trial-length / unlock framing as a monetization lever (directional).
  • REF Peloton (8534) / Spotify (9410) — trial-badge-led tier cards as a convention pattern.

Risk

Trials can lower net revenue if trial→paid is weak and they cannibalize would-be direct buyers — a real risk for impulse dating purchases at a warm wall. Mitigate: keep the trial short (3 days), test against the no-trial control on net paid revenue, and segment by like-count / intent.

4 Convention Check

Measured against the dating-paywall archetype (Tinder family) and the cross-category paywall conventions in the corpus. The rule: don't recommend a convention the target already uses unless we change how it's used.

✓ Already present

  • Locked-feature / feature-wall framing
  • Enumerated premium benefits
  • Multi-tier plan cards (Gold / Platinum)
  • Single primary CTA + restore + legal
  • Per-period pricing

→ H1, H2, H3 deliberately change how these are used (hero dominance, loss-framed grid, annual anchor), not whether they exist.

△ Missing vs. strong paywalls

  • True comparison grid with explicit "unchecked at lower tier" rows (have: Weather Channel, Perplexity, WaPo)
  • Annual default + per-week unit pricing + savings badge (have: Audible, Babbel, Reader)
  • Visible risk reversal — trial / intro term with inline condition (have: Quizlet, Peloton, Spotify)
  • Personalized social proof tied to the live like-count

◇ Unusual / category-specific

  • Monthly-as-default is unusual among high-ARPU subscription apps, which tend to anchor annual — a deliberate dating-app impulse-pricing choice worth A/B-revisiting (H3).
  • The trigger feature (see-who-likes-you) is uniquely personalizable — most categories can't show the user their own locked content. H1 exploits this edge.
  • Countdown urgency (BetterMe, Loona) is common elsewhere but risky on a warm intent moment — see divergent option D2.

5 Divergent Options

Bolder mechanisms transferred from adjacent categories. Use the mechanism, not the literal design. These are higher-variance — run only after the core four.

D1 · Stronger %-off anchor on annual Lazyweb

From Loona (sc_id 22924) & BetterMe (90747): a strong percent-off anchor ("First-timer 50% off") let the paywall raise the discounted price while keeping a simple accept/decline. Transfer: frame Platinum's annual as a one-time "new-to-Premium" discount so the higher tier reads as a deal, lifting top-tier ARPU.

Risk: discount-trained users may wait for offers; reserve for the upgrade (not re-subscribe) moment.

D2 · Scarcity on the reward, not the price Lazyweb

Countdown urgency (BetterMe 90747, JustFit 8060) usually targets price. Transfer: apply urgency to the reward instead — "{N} of your likes are from people active in the last 24h" — which is honest, ties to the warm moment, and avoids dark-pattern price timers that erode trust on a relationship product.

Risk: must be truthful and dynamic; a fake/static timer would damage brand trust badly in dating.
