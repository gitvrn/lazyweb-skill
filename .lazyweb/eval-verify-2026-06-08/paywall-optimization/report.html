Redesign hypotheses

These lead the report. Each one is a change to this paywall, anchored to a named friction above. Experiment evidence (Section 5) validates them; it did not generate them. Learnings are directional (screenshot-diff), not measured lift.

H1 — Make the trial mechanics explicit with a 3-step timeline attacks F1

Making the 7-day trial legible — replacing the bare "Start free trial" with a Today → Day 5 reminder → Day 7 billing timeline plus a reminder toggle, directly above the CTA — should lift trial-start rate (and therefore paid conversion) on this cold-session paywall because it removes the "when do I get charged / how do I stop it" anxiety that makes first-session users dismiss a hard paywall.

Expected metric: trial-start rate → paid conversion. Guardrail: trial-to-paid retention / refund volume (a timeline that over-promises easy cancellation could raise churn).

Implementation: Insert a 3-node horizontal/vertical timeline between the benefits list and the CTA: Today — full premium access; Day 5 — we remind you before any charge (with an on-by-default reminder toggle); Day 7 — your annual plan starts, cancel anytime. Pull "cancel anytime" out of the fine print and onto the timeline.

Supporting evidence: The timeline-explainer is a dominant Health & Fitness convention this paywall is missing (betterme, boldvoice, coinsnap, mimo — Section 5). AllTrails (269c25ae) swapped a discount gate for a 7-day trial with a timeline; learning: benefit-led trial framing reduces commitment risk while keeping the promise near the action.

Risk: Adds vertical height; on small devices the timeline must not push the CTA below the fold. Low conceptual risk — it is the category default.

H2 — Justify the defaulted annual plan with an effective rate + visible monthly anchor attacks F2

Making the defaulted annual plan self-justifying — showing the effective per-week (or per-day) price as the hero number and keeping a struck-through monthly equivalent visible as an anchor — should lift annual-plan share and overall paid conversion because the annual price currently lands as one large unexplained number with nothing to make it feel small or like a deal.

Expected metric: annual-plan share + paid conversion. Guardrail: monthly-plan cannibalization / overall ARPU.

Implementation: Reframe the annual card so the dominant line is "$X.XX / week" (annual price ÷ 52) with the full "$YY billed yearly" beneath it, and render a monthly figure beside it as a faded anchor showing the per-week saving (e.g. "vs $Z/wk monthly — save N%"). Keep annual pre-selected. This does not add a plan; it reframes the one that is already defaulted.

Supporting evidence: Impulse (7d8eb52e) reframed its plan to "$39.99/yr · $0.77/wk" while keeping weekly visible as an anchor; learning: a low effective weekly rate reduces price resistance while the weekly plan anchors the comparison. Savings-badge + monthly/annual anchoring is a near-universal convention in references (reader, gentler, peloton, myfitnesspal — Section 5) that this paywall omits.

Risk: Surfacing a cheaper monthly anchor could pull some users off annual; mitigate by keeping annual pre-selected and the per-week delta prominent.

H3 — Lead with the user's just-configured fasting outcome, not a generic benefits list attacks F3

Making the hero headline reflect the specific goal/protocol the user selected in onboarding — instead of a generic premium-benefits list — should lift paid conversion because this post-onboarding paywall is selling an unproven promise to a cold user, and a personalized outcome ("Your 16:8 plan to reach [goal] by [date]") makes the promise feel earned rather than boilerplate.

Expected metric: paid conversion (CTA tap rate). Guardrail: trial-to-paid retention (a personalized promise must be deliverable or it inflates refunds).

Implementation: Replace the static hero/benefits headline with a templated outcome string populated from onboarding answers (chosen fasting window, weight or wellness goal, target timeframe). Demote the generic benefits to a supporting checklist beneath the personalized headline.

Supporting evidence: Apple Fitness (47238428) shifted from trial-first to benefit/outcome-led activation copy; learning: selling the outcome before the subscription terms can lift conversion while keeping the annual upsell intact. Gentler (40106370) found broader, expanded outcome framing supported a higher price.

Risk: Requires onboarding answers to be available at paywall render; needs a sensible fallback headline when they are missing. Distinct mechanism from H1/H2 (personalized value, not trial clarity or price framing).

Divergent options

Bolder mechanisms to hold in reserve — transferred for their mechanism, not their literal design.

D1 — Add a soft "See all plans / billing options" escape instead of relying on the close X attacks F4

The target's only non-commit path is the top-left close, forcing an all-or-nothing decision on a cold user. Zero (the closest fasting analog) keeps a "More billing options" link, and JustFit's experiment (258ef49b) found a clear trial-bypass reduces purchase friction. Mechanism: give a hesitant user a cheaper monthly/longer-trial landing instead of a bounce. Why not a core hypothesis: it risks diluting annual share (H2's goal), so it is a guarded experiment, not a default ship. Keep close affordance, add the link as the secondary action.

D2 — Countdown / first-timer urgency on the annual price

JustFit (258ef49b) and Loona (1e83e388, a stronger 50% first-timer anchor) lean on urgency + a percent-off anchor to lift conversion and let the discounted annual price rise. Mechanism: a one-time post-onboarding discount with a visible expiry converts the "I'll decide later" user now. Caution: urgency on a cold first session can read as pressure for a niche/values-driven fasting audience; test against a calm-framing control before adopting.
